Japan April 8 & 11, 2026

Himeji, Kobe & Hiroshima

Himeji

A friend recommended I visit Himeji Castle while I was in Osaka, since it was close.

Unlike some castles in Japan (like ones in Osaka and Kyoto) that were destroyed at some point in the past and rebuilt — sometimes multiple times — the one in Himeji stands more or less as it was built in the very early 1600s. It's known as the White Heron castle because of its exterior paint job. One interesting thing about Himeji is that while it has never been destroyed like other castles, it has been dismantled — yes, dismantled — and put back together again as part of a restoration project.

1 / 8

It was really spectacular on a clear day with the cherry blossoms. And, you can walk through the main keep (you get a bag to put your shoes in and slippers if you need them) and other parts of the complex.

1 / 8

And there are lots of really beautiful gardens and areas with foliage, too. Himeji was really a highlight of the Japan trip.

Kobe

On the way back to Osaka from Himeji, I stopped in Kobe. I was really only there for about an hour, so unfortunately there's not much to report. But Kobe has a little Chinatown right near a train stop, so I walked around it and the nearby shōtengai and got some food. There was a line outside this pork bun place, so I figured it must be good, and it was. Ate them too fast to take a pic.

Hiroshima

Another day I took the Shinkansen, aka the bullet train, to Hiroshima to meet an old pal from Olympia and visit the Peace Memorial Museum. The train I took apparently reaches speeds of 300 km/h, or about 186 mph, during the trip. Here's a video of it pulling into the Hiroshima station.

The Peace Memorial Museum advocates for the abolition of nuclear weapons. So I saw the Pearl Harbor memorial in Hawaii and the Hiroshima memorial within a couple of weeks of each other. There were way too many people packed into the museum the day we were there, but the effect of the exhibits was still very powerful.

Since World War II, Hiroshima has become home to over a million people. And obviously the city had a long history before then, so there's a castle there that we checked out. It's a rebuild from the 1950s, and looks different from Himeji's because it isn't covered in white plaster. We also took an hour-long archery class they offer on the grounds.

There was a Hiroshima Carp baseball game happening in the stadium right next to the castle while we were there and we could hear the crowd cheering and chanting the whole time. The game let out about the same time as I headed back to the train station so I walked back in a sea of baseball fans.
