Japan April 6, 2026

Kyoto

The first day trip I took was to Kyoto, which from the 790s CE until the 1860s served as the imperial throne. It's definitely worth more than a day trip, but I did what I could this time. The petals were starting to fall from the cherry blossoms, so here are some of those drifting in some little waves in the Kamo River in Kyoto.

My main stop was Fushimi Inari Taisha, which is the head shrine for Inari Okami, a top deity of the native Japanese religion called Shinto. There's a lot more to learn about all that and if you're interested, you can start here. But the short version is that the shrine was founded in 711 and is home to Inari Okami, "a deity who feeds, clothes and houses us and protects us so that all of us may live with abundance and pleasure" and "a deity who protects all people from the Emperor to the common people so that we all may live happily and with abundance," according to the shrine's website.

There are tens of thousands of other Inari shrines across Japan but this is the main one, apparently. And there are many shrines within this one. Here are some of those.

There are also thousands of torii, which are gates (very often red or red and black) that mark the entrance to a sacred place throughout the shrine.

1 / 6

It's also home to a bamboo forest and beautiful gardens. Although you are supposed to watch out for wild boars…

1 / 4

Foxes are said to be messengers of Inari Okami. Not actual foxes, but spiritual ones, according to the shrine. There are statues of foxes all over the shrine and many of the tourist shops around it sell fox-themed items, including fox-face pastries and other sweets.

1 / 4

The shrine is located at the base of Mt. Inari, or Inariyama, a small mountain you can climb to the top of. There used to be a temple at the top, but now there's a shrine to the temple that was once there. It's a very nice hike up there.

1 / 5

There are also lots of cool things to visit right around Fushimi Inari Taisha, including other shrines and temples and nice neighborhoods. I saw a burnt cherry tree that they are rehabilitating.

1 / 13

There is some interesting coexistence between ancient and modern times. Here are some random shots from a little industrial area near the mountain, some anti-littering signs and a little collection of Tanuki, which are magical versions of Japanese raccoon dogs.

And then there is Fushimi Castle, which is a 1960s construction of one that used to be in this location in Kyoto. It is really impressive, and it just lives in the neighborhood and towers over a nearby baseball/soccer field.

I had a nice walk back to the train as the sun was going down.
