Hawaii March 28–April 1, 2026

Maui

I spent my final few days in Hawaiʻi on Maui, and it was every bit as epic as those on the big island and Oʻahu. As with the other islands, the recent big rains had caused a lot of damage on Maui and nearby Molokaʻi. And all the rivers were running above normal because of it, too.

As I mentioned in my first post, I got into watching surfing videos during the pandemic, but one of the first surfing films I ever saw that really captured my imagination was Riding Giants, which includes a lot about the Peʻahi wave, also known as Jaws. In big swells it can reach 60 to 70 feet in height. And people surf it.

So of course that was on my Maui to-see list. Like my earlier visit to Pipeline on Oʻahu, Peʻahi was not big at all when I saw it, but it was still really cool to be there. You hike about a mile down a really deeply rutted dirt road with huge mud puddles to get to the cliffs overlooking the ocean, and then you can continue all the way down to the beach if you want to.

And I never knew this, but there's a freshwater stream that runs down through a gorge into the sea right there, it's called the Uaoa stream.

The hike was muddy and rocky but fine overall. In these pictures you can see some of the furcraea foetida, aka Mauritius hemp, that is one of many invasive species in Hawaiʻi.

I stopped at a couple of other neat places as I drove around Maui, including the Hoʻokipa Beach overlook, where I saw a surfer and a bodyboarder doing their things.

And Twin Falls, a private park that had three waterfalls (one of which was closed because of dangerous conditions).

And then, last but very much not least, at the end of this day I visited Haleakalā National Park.

Here's a view from the road up to the summit.

At the top of the mountain — when the weather is clear — you have amazing views of the big island, Molokaʻi and Lanai.

Native Hawaiians historically trekked and still trek up to the top in order to teach and learn about astronomically-based navigation. And in modern times scientists have built several space observation posts there with all kinds of telescopes and other instruments.

And a really neat thing you can do at the summit is watch the sun rise or set. I just happened to be there a couple of hours before sundown, so I waited to see it, and it was sure worth it. We even got a "second sunset," where the sun looks like it goes down behind some clouds, but then reappears beneath them before it actually sets over the ocean.
