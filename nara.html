Japan April 11, 2026

Nara

Nara was a great day trip from Osaka, it's another town with a long history and was at one time the capital, but the main reason I wanted to go is because it's home to the Mt. Kasuga (Kasugayama) Primeval Forest. They call it that because they prohibited logging in the area in 841 CE. So there are some really big trees in the forest as well as interesting critters, buddhas carved into caves, waterfalls, shrines and other sights.

So I'll get to that. But first, a lot of people go to the park at the foothills of Kasugayama because there are tons of small sika deer that are habituated to humans, like squirrels or pigeons or something. The deer are protected for both spiritual and conservation reasons. And people can feed the deer by hand and take pictures with them, so it's a really popular tourist spot. Feeding the deer can get a little hectic sometimes, though, as you can see from this video…

And you should definitely be on guard against deer attacks.

1 / 6

I didn't do the deer feeding thing but it was funny to watch for a minute. The deer naturally have horns, so there's an annual horn-cutting ritual. There's also deer shit everywhere, of course. Not as bad as trodding through a Canada goose habitat, but in that ballpark — you're getting shit on your shoes, no two ways about it. So I was happy to get up into the forest.

But along the trail, they warn you about bears … and snakes … and bears and snakes … and poisonous mushrooms … and leeches …

1 / 5

Fortunately I didn't encounter any representatives of these species.

On a side note, Google translate's camera function has been awesome for all kinds of situations. And when that hasn't worked, my iPhone's camera has also been able to translate text in pictures, which I didn't even know it could do.

Anyway, the hike in the forest was really awesome, another major highlight of the trip for me. I was the only one at the Uguisu waterfall…

1 / 2

Here's an abandoned shrine near the waterfall and some Buddhas carved into the mountain, called magaibutsu, that date to the 1100s.

And I saw a couple of critters, but I have not been able to identify this frog on the internet. The rest of its mates in the pond were extremely wary of my presence and I'd see or hear them jumping in the water when I'd be like 15 feet down the path. This one hung out and let me take its picture, though. Maybe they draw straws and take turns indulging photographers. Maybe this one lost a bet. I dunno. But all of them made this really funny noise when they jumped, it was not a croak at all, it was like a little frogish purr? Which I can only describe as cute. They'd go, "prrr prrr" (roll your rs for the full effect) and jump in the water.

1 / 2

And this thing, I'm pretty sure, is called an anaguma, or Japanese badger, and it was not phased by me at all, it just kept rooting around and then eventually went on its way. Apparently they are very shy so I was pretty lucky to stumble across it. I filmed it for about a minute and a half, this is just a clip. There are other mammals called tanuki on the mountain that kind of look like it, but the internet told me this is the badger.

The Kasuga Taisha shrine is also in the park, and dates back to the 700s CE, when Nara was the seat of imperial power in Japan. Like the primeval forest, it's a UNESCO World Heritage site.

1 / 8

I wasn't even planning on going to Nara but someone on Reddit recommended it, and I'm really glad I took the trip. Reddit's been a help too, people post about their travels in real time so you can kind of ask questions or get tips while you're out and about that way.
