Hawaii March 23–27, 2026

Oʻahu

All right! Here goes with the blog…

My first stop was Oʻahu, Hawaiʻi. I stayed in Honolulu, very near Waikīkī, which I learned was, for just one year, the first capital of the Kingdom of Hawaiʻi in the late 1700s. It was also a retreat for Hawaiian royals, who liked to surf there. Queens Beach was also very nearby, and I actually swam there instead of Waikīkī.

I arrived on Monday, March 23, which was not very long after the state got slammed by a very powerful "Kona low" storm, which caused massive flooding and damage in many parts of Oʻahu and also severely impacted the other islands. Honolulu got lots of flooding, too, and I saw plenty of evidence of it, although it was drying out pretty well by the time I got there.

I went up to Manoa Falls, which is just outside Honolulu, and you can see the water was still running strong all over the place.

1 / 3

I really enjoyed the Bishop Museum in Honolulu, which does a great job of walking a visitor through the history of the Hawaiian islands from the earliest days of human settlers, who sailed there from other parts of Polynesia, through the battles between the islands, their unification under one leader, and then the government-sanctioned coup by U.S. and European business leaders that eliminated the Hawaiian monarchy (which the U.S. government apologized for in 1993).

I should have taken more pictures there — this is apparently the only one I took. It's a model of a part of a Hawaiian village.

I also ate great Hawaiian food, visited other sites in Honolulu, and appreciated the nature in the area (except for the feral chickens, which were cute until the roosters started crowing at 3 a.m. every morning on every island). Honolulu's a big city and they've got the same issues as most U.S. big cities these days. I appreciated the bus service there.

One day I went out to the Pearl Harbor National Memorial. I grew up pretty interested in WWII history and studied it a bit in college. There's a museum that has lots of information about the base and the attack on it as well as some pretty crazy damaged aircraft and other items. Lots of personal stories and context. You can go out and see one of the ships they couldn't salvage, the U.S.S. Arizona, and visit the separate memorial they have there. They said some guys who survived, when they finally died many years later, were cremated and laid to rest with their mates who didn't survive.

I tried to get around Oʻahu a bit, too, so one day I rented a car and drove around the island. I started out going to the North Shore, which served as a bit of inspiration for the name of this blog.

For a while, "Today's Waves" — or "Todayz Wavez" depending on your mood — was just one of a bunch of probably appropriately never-used song or album title ideas, but there was something about the name I liked and it kept eddying around in my mind.

Some of the idea came from my fascination with surfing videos, which I started watching during the pandemic and provided some welcome escape from NYC life at the time. Some of the videos I watched involved waves in Hawaii, of course, including the famous Banzai Pipeline wave at ʻEhukai Beach on the North Shore.

I had never surfed, but the videos inspired me so much that I tried it for the first time in 2023. And it was really fun! That remains the only time I've done it, but it's now kind of an option, depending on circumstances, I suppose.

On my drive through the North Shore, I saw relief efforts happening all over the place related to the Kona low storm. The local communities had created their own hubs where they were distributing food, water and other necessities. Lots of businesses and schools were still closed, and many roads were closed or hard to pass because of mud. I saw a few people actively cleaning out the ruined contents of their homes.

But I made it to ʻEhukai Beach, although as you'll see here, Pipeline was not pumping in any way shape or form.

I should say, the idea of "Today's Waves" was only partially inspired by surfing, and I really was thinking of all the other varieties of wave forms that we experience in different ways, like sound waves, brain waves, light waves (and particles of course), new waves, waves of emotion … and maybe sometimes you just do the wave — channeling that kinetic energy from the tips of your fingers on one arm all the way through your body out the tips of the fingers on the other side.

Anyway, before I got to Pipeline I stopped at Waimea Bay and the Waimea Valley Park, where I checked out Waimea Falls, which had lots of water, like Manoa Falls. I got to swim in the little pool.

Driving around the north side of the island from east to west, I also got to see all kinds of other really beautiful beaches and mountains. A really great spot was the Nuʻuanu Pali lookout, which gives you a view of Kailua.

OK, that's it for Oʻahu. The big island and Maui are next, but this took a while so not sure how long those will be, haha.
