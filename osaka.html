Japan April 1–15, 2026

Osaka

I got a little apartment in Osaka with a kitchen and washing machine to stay at during my two-week Japan stay. Osaka is the third-most populous city in Japan with about 2.8 million people, after Tokyo and Yokohama, and it's part of the Keihanshin Metropolitan Area, which includes Kobe, Kyoto and the surrounding areas. The whole area is home to about 19 million people. Osaka is an economic hub and a really big port city.

My place was right by the Yodo River, which is the main river that runs through the city and it was great because I could go walking there whenever I wanted.

In an unplanned but awesome coincidence, I just happened to get to Osaka very close to the peak of the cherry blossoms, or sakura, season. I've seen the cherry blossoms in Olympia, New York and D.C., but there were so many in Osaka and all around the area, they kind of dominated the environment. And people are really into it, celebrating it with hanami, which is kind of a party under the trees, and going out and just taking them in and photographing them. People reflect on the transient beauty of the blossoms and how many things in life are like that. I spent a decent amount of time just walking up and down Kema Sakuranomiya Park, which is lined with cherry trees.

I did a ton of walking around in general, trying to get a bit of a sense of the city while also seeing cool things, like the big castle in the middle of town. Osaka castle was originally built in the 1580s, but then it burned down a couple of times in the 1600s and apparently was never rebuilt, until in the 1930s the government decided to fund a reconstruction. So that's the building that stands there now. The castle is directly across from one of the very early imperial palaces, too, which is no longer there, although the grounds are kind of open to check out. There's a cool history museum on the upper floors of a nearby building that lets you see out onto the landscape and has all the models and short films you need to imagine what the palace was like in those days.

Osaka is a lively city, especially at night, there are tons of streets called shōtengai, which are really long, covered arcades that have all kinds of different shops. Some of the most popular of those are in the Minami section of town, and it's neat to walk around there. Lots of colorful lights and shops and restaurants and people everywhere.

Because I was in Osaka for two weeks, I could kind of get a little bit of a daily routine going, which usually started with getting coffee at a konbini, or convenience store. I also bought a lot of food at these places, they have pre-prepared meals for pretty cheap prices, like half or less than half of what you'd pay at a restaurant. It was a nice option to have. And they are everywhere, you can't throw a rock without hitting one. But of course I also hit regular grocery stores, restaurants and food stands.

I was fortunate to have a friend who lives in Osaka, so he took me out a couple of times to places that he plays music at (shoutout to Deron, hear his music here). I did not know you can still smoke in bars and restaurants that allow it, but you can there.

Another awesome park I went to was Tsurumi-Ryokuchi Park, which was used for the 1990 Horticultural Expo, the theme of which was "The Harmonious Coexistence of Nature and Mankind." It's really neat, I guess they asked participating countries to design their own gardens, so there's a part of the park where Japan, Ireland, Yugoslavia (!), Italy, Morocco, etc. all have one.

1 / 9

Overall I got a really nice impression of Osaka and Kansai. I know you never get to know what it's really like to live somewhere when you're just visiting but I found the people to be nice, the food good, things weren't crazy expensive, and there was lots to learn, see and do. The public transportation was great, it's all I used to get anywhere the whole time I was there.
