Landing in Tokyo: First Hours in a City That Never Slows Down

The plane banked over Tokyo Bay just after dawn and I pressed my face to the window like a child.

Nothing prepares you for the scale of Tokyo. Grid upon grid upon grid, stretching to every horizon, interrupted only by the occasional glint of the Imperial Palace grounds. From altitude it looks impossible — too large to be real, too ordered to be human.

Then you land and the scale collapses into something intimate: a taxi driver with white gloves, a highway lined with cherry trees coming into bloom, the low hum of a city that has already been awake for hours.

Arriving at the Hotel

I had booked a small room in Yanaka, one of the few neighborhoods that survived the firebombing of 1945. The streets are narrow, tiled, flanked by old wooden shopfronts selling pickles and incense and ceramics. It feels like Tokyo reaching back into itself.

There's a Japanese phrase, shoganai — loosely, "it cannot be helped." Tokyo teaches you this the first morning, when the jet lag wakes you at 3am and the city is already making coffee.

The First Meal

I found ramen at 7am in a basement counter with eight seats. The broth had been simmering since the previous night. The cook, a man in his sixties, said nothing and handed me a small towel. We understood each other perfectly.
